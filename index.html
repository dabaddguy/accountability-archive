Brady Motion / Brady Disclosure
Brady v. Maryland (1963) - The prosecution MUST disclose ALL evidence favorable to you, including evidence that could impeach prosecution witnesses or reduce your sentence.
When to Use: ‚Ä¢ ANY criminal case - request this IMMEDIATELY
‚Ä¢ When police/prosecutors might hide exculpatory evidence
‚Ä¢ To expose officer misconduct & false statements
‚Ä¢ Before trial, during discovery phase
What You Get: ‚Ä¢ Police disciplinary records
‚Ä¢ Prior false statements by officers
‚Ä¢ Evidence of planted evidence or coercion
‚Ä¢ Witness deals with prosecution
‚Ä¢ Any evidence suggesting innocence
Giglio Motion
Giglio v. United States (1972) - Requires disclosure of ANY deals, promises, or benefits given to prosecution witnesses in exchange for testimony.
When to Use: ‚Ä¢ When informants or cooperating witnesses testify
‚Ä¢ To expose paid informants
‚Ä¢ To reveal plea deals made with witnesses
‚Ä¢ Shows if witness got charges dropped for testifying
Exposes: ‚Ä¢ Money paid to witnesses
‚Ä¢ Reduced charges for cooperation
‚Ä¢ Immunity agreements
‚Ä¢ Any benefit given for testimony
‚Ä¢ Secret deals between prosecutors & witnesses

‚Ä¢ Access internal affairs investigations

Franks Hearing / Motion
Franks v. Delaware (1978) - Challenge search warrants based on police lies or omissions. If police lied to get the warrant, ALL evidence gets SUPPRESSED and your case may be DISMISSED.
When to Use: ‚Ä¢ Police lied in search warrant affidavit
‚Ä¢ Officer omitted key facts to mislead judge
‚Ä¢ Warrant based on unreliable informant
‚Ä¢ Evidence was illegally obtained
‚Ä¢ Cops fabricated probable cause
Result If You Win: ‚Ä¢ ALL evidence suppressed (thrown out)
‚Ä¢ Case may be DISMISSED
‚Ä¢ Exposes police perjury
‚Ä¢ Can lead to officer prosecution
‚Ä¢ Opens door to ¬ß 1983 lawsuit
Bivens Action (Sue Federal Agents)
Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents (1971) - Sue FEDERAL agents (FBI, DEA, ICE, Border Patrol, etc.) for constitutional violations. Like ¬ß 1983 but for federal officers.
When to Use: ‚Ä¢ FBI, DEA, ATF, Secret Service misconduct
‚Ä¢ Border patrol violations
‚Ä¢ Federal prison guard abuse
‚Ä¢ Fourth Amendment violations by feds
‚Ä¢ Excessive force by federal agents
Important Note: Supreme Court has limited Bivens in recent years - consult a lawyer. But it STILL applies for Fourth Amendment (search/seizure) and Fifth Amendment (due process) violations. Don't give up your rights.

Challenge unlawful detention or imprisonment. If you're being held illegally, this forces the government to JUSTIFY it in court or RELEASE you. One of the oldest protections against tyranny.
When to Use: ‚Ä¢ Wrongfully convicted
‚Ä¢ Evidence of actual innocence
‚Ä¢ Ineffective assistance of counsel
‚Ä¢ Constitutional violations at trial
‚Ä¢ Held past release date
‚Ä¢ Detention without charges

‚Ä¢ Pre-trial detention challenges
‚Ä¢ Constitutional right to challenge detention

‚Ä¢ Department trains officers to violate rights
‚Ä¢ City has unconstitutional policy
‚Ä¢ Failure to discipline bad officers
‚Ä¢ Inadequate supervision
‚Ä¢ Department culture encourages abuse

Miranda v. Arizona (1966) - Police MUST inform you of your rights before custodial interrogation. If they don't, your statements get THROWN OUT. Know your rights and USE them.
When to Use: ‚Ä¢ Police questioned you without reading rights
‚Ä¢ You were in custody but not advised
‚Ä¢ Continued questioning after you invoked rights
‚Ä¢ Trickery to avoid giving Miranda warnings
‚Ä¢ Coerced confession

‚Ä¢ Attorney provided if you can't afford one
‚Ä¢ Anything you say CAN be used against you

‚Ä¢ Find prior cases with similar facts
‚Ä¢ Argue officer KNEW conduct was unlawful
‚Ä¢ Show violation was OBVIOUS (excessive force, false arrest)
‚Ä¢ Use video evidence
Key Strategies: ‚Ä¢ Cite circuit court precedent
‚Ä¢ Use Supreme Court cases
‚Ä¢ Show egregious conduct
‚Ä¢ Prove officer training showed them it was wrong
‚Ä¢ Use officer's own testimony against them
‚Ä¢ Establish custom/policy (Monell claim)
